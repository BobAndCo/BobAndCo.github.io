Geometry of Physics

This is a reading group with the physics student union (PhySU) at the University of Toronto that I am conducting for students who are interested in mathematical physics. It covers the main geometry that is used in the subject. We intend on covering the following topics:

  • A breif introduction to Group Theory
  • A breif introduction to Topology
  • Linear and Multilinear algebra
  • Derivative and Integral Theory
  • Introduction to Smooth Manifolds
  • Introduction to Fibre Bundles
  • Introduction To (Psuedo)Riemannian Geometry
  • Introduction to Symplectic Geometry
  • Classical Physics through Smooth Manifolds
  • Spinors
  • Dirac Operator and Spinor motion on Manifolds

Here are the handwritten lecture notes with a breif desciption of what they cover. I am currently (as of January 26, 2026) working on typing these up as well and when I am done, I will post them here. One can find problem sheets at the bottom. Finally I want to mention that I am recording the meetings of this reading group and they can be found on my youtube here.

  • Lecture 1 - We talked about what I want to cover. The definition of a group, some classical groups, some examples of groups used in physics and examples of how symmetries correspond to conservation laws. What group homomorphism are. Definition of representations.

  • Lecture 2 - We talked the definition of a group action, recapped the definition of representations, talked about quantum spin \(\frac{1}{2}\)-particles and the Stern-Gerlach experiments, the pauli matrices, talked about how to embed \(\R^3\) in a matrix space and introduced the action of \(\textrm{SU}(2)\) on \(\R^3\) via conjugations (spin action)

  • Lecture 3 - We talked about and motivated the definition of a topological space (open and closed sets), sequences and convergence in a topological space, properties of topological spaces such as T2, 2nd countable and compact. Furthermore we introduced the notation of a continuous map. We introduced the notion of a topological manifold. Finally we introduced an intimate connection between pointed topological spaces and groups via the (first) fundamental group functor.

  • Lecture 4 - We talked about and motivated the definition the definitions of Bosonic and Fermionic exchange statistics we see in 3 dimensions. We talked about the fundamental group. We defined (the ordered and unordered) configuration spaces and how the fundamental group of the unordered fundamental group has a natural action on state space that induces exchange statistics. Finally, we talked about the allowed unitary scalar representations of the groups \(S_n\) and \(B_n\) and how these lead to the theoretical existence of Anyons.

  • Lecture 5 - We talked about tensor products of general vector spaces. We saw various isomorphisms between vector spaces and their duals. We say how we can talk about tensor product spaces as multiparticle states and multilinear maps. We found the transformation laws for the tensors and their components. We motivated why we want to talk about maps like the determinant.

  • Lecture 6 - We talked about the complexification of a vector space and we also defined an almost complex struture on even dimensional real vector spaces. We saw how the notion of complex and almost complex on vector spaces is the same. We further talked about the alternation map and defined the wedge product on the space of forms. We talked about inner products in a more general setting and we defined an extension of an inner product onto the various tensor spaces. We finally defined the Hodge star operator on the space of k-forms.

  • Lecture 7 - We talked about Hilbert space, Banach spaces, symmetric tensors and the symmetric product. We also talked about how symmetric tensors are homogenous polynomials of vector spaces. Finally we talked about vector valued tensors and Lie algebras.

  • Lecture 8 - We talked about why calculus is defined over the reals and not the rationals, this gave us motivation to then restrict ourselves to complete spaces with norms (in other words Banach spaces). We then defined the derivative of a banach space valued curve and used it to generalize to the case of directional derivatives called Gâteaux variations. We used this to define the Gâteaux derivative of a map between arbitrary banach spaces. We then discussed how this notion is too weak and gave an example of a Gâteaux differentiable function that is not continuous. We used this to define Frechét derivatives and we defined functions of class \(C^n\). We concluded with the three main(hard) theorems of Frechét calculus that prove to use their use. These were the chain rule, inverse function theorem and the implicit function theorem.

  • Lecture 9 - We motivated the need for integration in many dimensions through looking at the work done by a force along a certain path. We then tried to talk about flux through a surface and ran into the problem of integration over \(\R^2\). We then motivated why the Riemann integral does not suffice for physics through an example by Henri Lebesgue. This then lead us to the notation of measure spaces and the notation of a measure. We defined the Borel sigma algebra of a topological space and defined the Lebesgue sigma algebra of \(\R^n\).

  • Lecture 10 - We developed the theory of Lebesgue integration of measureable functions on sigma finite measure spaces. We talked about \(L^p\) spaces. We also talked about the Monotone Convergence Theorem (MCT) and the Dominated Convergence Theorem (DCT).

  • Lecture 11 - We further developed the theory of Lebesgue integration on sigma finite measure spaces. We talked about the product measure, iterated integrals and saw how to compute integrals over product spaces using Tonelli's and Fubini's Theorem.

  • Lecture 12 - We saw how to integrate over surfaces in 2d. Moreover we say that all the theorems that look like FTC follow the exact same pattern which gives us motivation to strive towards a generalization of these. Moreover we say that a lot of the constructions in specific dimensions seem arbitrary and use the dimensionality of the space a lot.

  • Lecture 13 - We introduced the ideas of catgory theory and give the basic definitions. We saw how categories are already things we have seen before and we gave a lot of examples of various categories. We defined the idea of a functor and natural transformation, though we will not really need to know what natural transformations are. We formalized the idea of a dual object using the opposite category.

  • Lecture 14 - We introduced the idea of smooth manifolds. We gave examples of smooth manifolds and defined smooth maps on these objects.

  • Lecture 15 - We introduced the ideas of tangent vectors, differentials of smooth functions, lie groups and their associated lie algebras. We also computed the differential of multiplication and inversion and we looked at the adjoint representations of the lie group and its lie algebra.

  • Lecture 16 - We saw how to reconstruct a manifold through the atlas. We used this to develop the idea of a tangent bundle. Finally we developed the idea of cocycles and used it to define vector bundles and fibre bundles.

  • Lecture 17 - We gave the definition of immersions, submersions and local diffeomorphisms. We motivated the regular level set theorem. We say a lot of examples of manifolds that are used in physics.

  • Lecture 18 - We found the tangent spaces of the manifold examples we saw the last. Moreover we gave a way of moving tangent spaces around on lie groups, more specifically we saw how to move the lie algebra around to get the tangent spaces at other places. for matrix groups. We saw how tangent vectors transformed to give motivation in defining cotangent vectors. We then defined the cotangent bundle and sections of vector bundles which gave rise to the definition of vector fields and differential 1-forms.

  • Lecture 19 - This time it was more discussion based but we talked about the other tensor bundles associated to the tangent bundle. We talked about local expressions of smooth tensor fields. We also saw how these tensor fields can be thought of as \(C^\infty(M)\)-linear maps. We introduced the Riemannian metric and symplectic form which are some of the most important types of tensor fields that we will worry about.

  • Lecture 20 - We talked about vector fields and flows. We defined the Lie bracket and the lie derivative and defined a relation between them.

  • Problem Sheet 2 - This problem sheet covers computations of derivatives and integrals in multiple definition, Picard-Lindelöf theorem and the relationship between complexification and symplectic vector spaces.

  • Problem Sheet 3 - This problem sheet covers ODEs, the matrix exponential, PDEs and the compoutations of the volume and the surface area of higher dimensional spheres and the area of a blob in \(\R^2\) with smooth boundary.

  • Problem Sheet 4 - This problem sheet covers Category theory, showing things are manifolds and geberal properties about Lie groups.

  • Problem Sheet 5 - This problem sheet covers the real and complex projective spaces, vector fields on manifolds, differential forms and integration.
