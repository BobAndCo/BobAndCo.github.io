Volume Forms Over Manifolds(and submanfolds)

Let us lead this post with some motivation. Suppose we were given the two dimensional submanifold in \(\R^2\) by \(\bar{B}(0; R)\) with the closed ball of radius \(R\) centered at the origin. Now we were tasked with finding the area of this manifold. Note that this amounts us to find the integral \[ v(\bar{B}(0; R)) = \int_{\bar{B}(0; R)}\mathrm{d}x \wedge dy \] But this integral is easy to compute since we can use polar change of variables to note that \[ \begin{align*} v(\bar{B}(0; R)) &= \int_{\bar{B}(0; R)}dx \wedge dy \\ &= \int_{0}^{R}\int_{0}^{2\pi}r\,d\theta dr \\ &= \int_{0}^{R}r\,dr\int_{0}^{2\pi}d\theta \\ &= \pi R^2 \end{align*} \] This is exactly what we expect. So is there a way to assign such a volume to every manifold? And the answer is that it is not that simple. With open submanifolds \(M\) of \(\R^n\) we can do the same procedure as above where we take the canonical volume form which we will call \(\omega = \mathrm{d}x^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm{d}x^n\) and the inclusion map \(i: M \to \R^n\) and note that \(i^*\omega \in \Omega^n(M)\). But the inclusion is just identity restricted to the submanifold and so the integal we perform is just over the region, just as above.

We should make clear what is a volume form on a manifold. After this we will answer the question of if every manifold admits one. After that we will discuss some canonical choices of volumes forms. Finally we will discuss some of the use cases of volume forms in the context of (psuedo)Riemannian manifolds

To make it clear, a volume form on a manifold \(M\) of dimension \(m\) is a top form \(\omega \in \Omega^m(M)\) such that for all \(p \in M\) we have that \(\omega_p \not\equiv 0\). In other words, at each \(p \in M\) there are vectors \(v_{p1}, \dots, v_{pm} \in T_pM\) such that \[ \omega_p(v_{1p}, \dots, v_{mp}) = 1 \] We may choose this to be one by rescaling the tangent vectors appropriately. This requirement seems trivial but as we will see below such differential forms dont always exist.

Since for each \(p \in M\) we have that \(\dim\bigwedge^mT_p^*M = 1\) we can take any nonzero tensor \(\omega_p\) and see that \(\text{span}\{\omega_p\} = \bigwedge^mT_p^*M\). Locally, i.e. on a chart (\(U, \varphi\)), we can pullback the volume form on \(\R^m\) back to a volume form on \(U\). We can check this by noting that the canonical volume form on \(\R^m\) is the determinant function on each tangent space and in local coordinates we have that \[ \varphi^*(\mathrm{d}x^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm{d}x^m) = \mathrm{d}\varphi^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm{d}\varphi^m \] but we know that \[ \mathrm{d}\varphi^i = \frac{\partial\varphi^i}{\partial y^j}\mathrm{d}x^j \] using summation convention, hence \[ \mathrm{d}\varphi^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm{d}\varphi^m = (\det D\varphi)\, \mathrm{d}y^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \mathrm{d}y^m \] and since the determinant is nonzero, given that \(\varphi\) is a diffeomorphism, we have that this is a volume form on \(U\). This shows that if \(M\) can be covered by one chart then there must be a volume form. Moreover this gives rise to a natural orientation on \(M\) since we may pullback the standand frame on \(\R^m\) to get an orientation. Let us explore this further.

Orientability and Volume Forms

First, what is meant by orientatibilty? We know from \(\R^3\) that the cross product gives us an orientation. In general we do not have such a structure. So given a vector space \(V\) with two ordered basis \(\{e_i\}\) and \(\{f_i\}\) we say that both are in the same orientation if the map sending \(e_i \mapsto f_i\) has a positive determinant. Now we note that this partitions the space of ordered bases of \(V\) into two parts. We may choose an orientation by choosing a basis on \(V\) and we will usually call is positively oriented.

On manifolds orientations are defined in a multitude of different ways. The way we will define it is by forcing the transition maps to be orientation preserving. What this means is that the determiant of the jacobian must be positive. If we are able to find an atlas for the manifold such that the transition maps are orientation preserving then we say that the manifold is orientable. Note that this is equivalent to choosing an orientation on each tangent space and forcing them to be part of the same orientation. With this we now have a construction we can form called the orientation cover of a manifold. For this construction we are free to let \(M\) be not connected by the connected case gives us an even more special object.

Let \(M\) be a connected manifold and let \[ \hat{M} = \{(p, O_p) \mid p \in M \text{ and } O_p \text{ is an orientation on } T_pM\} \] along with the projection map \(\pi: \hat{M} \to M\). Now note that this is infact a smooth double covering space of \(M\). The importance of this object comes from the fact that the orientatibility of \(M\) comes down to the connectedness of this space. To see this intuitively note that if \(\hat{M}\) is connected then there is a path from a positive orientation at a point to a negative one. But this path gives a path in \(GL(n, \R)\) with the basis being with respect to the orientation, the actual choice is not important. But this path has the property that it goes from maps having positive determinant to having negative determiant. But note that there cannot be a continous path like that. Hence the orientatibilty of a manifold comes down to the connectedness of its orientation cover.

Suppose (\(M, \omega\)) is a smooth manifold with \(\omega\) a volume form on \(M\). Is it true that \(M\) must be orientable? Let us try to figure this out. Note first that at each point \(p \in M\) we have that \(\omega_p\) defines an orientation. To see this note that if we take two ordered basis \(v_1, \dots, v_m\) and \(w_1, \dots, w_m\) of \(T_pM\) and let the change of basis matrix be \(A\) such that \(Av_i = w_i\) then note that \(\omega_p(w_1, \dots, w_m) = \omega_p(Av_1, \dots, Av_m) = \det(A)\omega_p(v_1, \dots, v_m)\). So now if \(\det(A) > 0\) then these bases are in the same orientation and if \(\det(A) < 0\) they are in the opposite. This means that the top form gives rise to a partition of the set of ordered bases in two, which we may take to be the orientation. So, at each point \(\omega\) forces a pointwise orientation of \(M\). Now to show that \(\omega\) defines an orientation on \(M\) we start by taking an open connected set \(U \subseteq M\) and a local frame \(\{X_i\}\) moreover, take the dual coframe \(\{X^i\}\). Now in this setting we have that \(\omega = f\,X^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge X^m\) this now means that \(\omega(X_1, \dots, X_m) = f\). Since \(\omega_p \neq 0\) for any \(p \in U\) we cannot have \(f(p) = 0\), hence on \(U\) f is either always positive or always negative (since \(U\) is connected). Hence we have a pointwise orientation, moreover the fact that locally this is always the same sign gives us the continuity of the pointwise orientation. This is all we needed, since these collections of local frame (up to changing sign for positive orientation) make up the defintion of an orienation on \(M\).

The exposition above shows that orientation and the existance of volume forms are one in the same thing. But can we make this stronger? By this I mean, suppose \(M\) is a oriented smooth manifold, does there exist a volume form \(\omega\)? We can deconstruct the argument above to define a collection of local forms. Then using a patition of unity note that the form may be defined globally. We must now show that this form is smooth. But this follows quickly as on each local frame we have defined a smooth form and the smooth combination of smooth forms is smooth. Hence we have a nowhere vanishing top form.

With all this out of the way, let me introduce my favorite case of existance. Suppose \(f: M \to \R\) is a smooth function with \(c \in \R\) a regular value. Then \(S = f^{-1}(c)\) is a regular submanifold of \(M\) of dimension \(\dim M - 1\). We now see that this submanifold is always orientable if \(M\) is.

Since Riemannian metrics always exist, we can choose one, and then define the gradient of the function \(f\). Now we will show that \(\text{grad}(f)\) is nowhere tangent to \(S\). From the defintion of the gradient we have that the gradient is the unique vector field such that \[ \langle\text{grad}(f), X\rangle = \mathrm{d}f(X) = Xf \] So now to show that \(\text{grad}(f)\) is nowhere tangent to \(S\), let \(\{X_i\}\) be a local orthonormal frame on \(S\) and since we are looking at \(S\) as a submanifold we idenity the tangent spaces appropriately. Let \(\{Y_i\}\) be a local orthonormal frame on \(M\) such that on \(S\) the first \(m-1\, Y_i\) are the \(X_i\) then in this case we have that \[ \text{grad}(f) = a^iY_i \] using the inner product characterization, on \(S\) we have that \[ a^i = \langle\text{grad}(f), X_i\rangle = \mathrm{d}f(X_i) = 0 \] for \(i = 1, \dots, m-1\). Therefore \(\text{grad}(f) \notin T_pS\) for all \(p \in S\). Now this vector field exists, moreover the existance of such a nowhere vanishing vector field is dependent only on the choice of Riemannian metric and Riemannian metrics always exist on any smooth manifolds. Hence we may take that such a vector field always exists. Let \(X\) be a vector field on \(M\) that is nowhere tangent to \(S\) then on \(S\) we have that \(\iota_X\omega\) is nowhere vanishing since at every point we can always take a basis of \(T_pS\) such that the collection including \(X\) gives a basis for \(T_pM\). So the form \(i^*\iota_X\omega \in \Omega^{m-1}(S)\) is a nowhere vanishing top form and hence is a volume form. From our above exposition this implies that \(S\) must be orientable.

From this we see that the existance of a nowhere vanishing vector field is enough to find an orientation on any \(m-1\) dimensional submanifold of \(M\). For \(0 < n \leq m\) it is sufficient to find \(m-n\) nowhere tangent and everywhere linearly independent to submanifold to find an orientation. These vector fields may merely be local frames as well.

Canonical Volume forms

There are two main structures that are used in the study of differential geometry, those are: Riemannian metrics, symplectic forms and Lie groups. Arguably the easiest one to discuss is the Lie group.

Lie Groups

So let \(G\) be a Lie group of dimension \(n\). Now let \(\omega_e \in \bigwedge^nT_eM\) be nonzero. Moreover note that \(\ell_g: G \to G\) defined by \(h \to gh\) is a diffeomorphism. Now at each point \(g \in G\) define \(\omega_g = \ell_{g^{-1}}^*\omega_e\). To see that this is a volume form, take a basis \(T_eG\), lets call it \(v_{1e}, \dots, v_{ne}\). Then \(\omega_e(v_{1e}, \dots, v_{ne}) \neq 0\), we choose a basis since this the \(\omega_e\) evaluates to something nonzero if and only if the collection is a basis. So now note that \(\mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}}:T_gG \to T_eG\) is an isomorphism, so there are unique tangent vectors \(v_{1g}, \dots, v_{ng} \in T_gG\) such that \((\mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}})_g(v_{ig}) = v_{ie}\). Note now that \[ \begin{align*} \omega_g(v_{1g}, \dots, v_{ng}) &= (\ell_{g^{-1}}^*\omega_e)_g(v_{1g}, \dots, v_{ng}) \\ &= \omega_e(\mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}}(v_{1g}), \dots, \mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}}(v_{ng})) \\ &= \omega_e(v_{1e}, \dots, v_{ne}) \\ &\neq 0 \end{align*} \] This shows that the \(\omega_g\) is nonzero. So \(\omega\) is a rough nonzero top form on \(G\). Now we must show that this form is actually smooth. To do this, let us make use of a global frame on \(G\). Let us choose a left invariant global frame \(\{X_i\}\), then we note that if \(\omega_e(X_1|_e, \dots, X_n|_e) = c \in \R\) then \[ \begin{align*} \omega_g(X_1|_g, \dots, X_n|_g) &= (\ell_{g^{-1}}^*\omega_e)_g(X_1|_g, \dots, X_n|_g) \\ &= \omega_e(\mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}}(X_1|_g), \dots, \mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}}(X_n|_g)) \\ &= \omega_e(X_1|_e, \dots, X_n|_e) \\ &= c \end{align*} \] where we need the left invariance to compute \(\mathrm{d}\ell_{g^{-1}}(X_i|_g)\). Since we have a global frame, every vector field \(X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) may be written as \(X = x^iX_i\) for some smooth functions \(x^i\). Finally let us take any collection of vector field \(\{Y_i\}_{i = 1}^{n}\) and let us consider the function \[ p \mapsto \omega_p(Y_1|_p, \dots, Y_n|_p) \] the form is smooth if and only if this function is smooth. But note that \[ \begin{align*} \omega_p(Y_1|_p, \dots, Y_n|_p) &= \omega_p(y_1^i(p)X_i|_p, \dots, y_n^i(p)X_i|_p) \\ &= y_1^{i_1}(p)\cdots y_n^{i_n}(p)\omega_p(X_{i_1}|_p, \dots, X_{i_n}|_p) \end{align*} \] but this is a product and sum of smooth functions, hence this form is smooth. From this we have seen a very important result, that every Lie group is orientable. Now if \(M\) is compact then we can choose this volume form in such a way that the volume of the Lie group under this volume form is \(1\). To do this, let \(\eta\) be the volume form as defined by the process above, then \(\int_M\eta\) is finite and hence define \(\omega = \frac{1}{\int_M\eta}\eta\)

Symplectic Manifolds

So let \((M ,\omega)\) be a symplectic manifold of dimension \(2m\). Now this means that at each point \(p \in M\) we have \(\omega_p\) is nondegenerate. If we take a symplectic basis \(v_{1p}, \dots, v_{mp}, w_{1p}, \dots, w_{mp}\) of \(T_pM\) then \[ \omega_p = v_{1p} \wedge w_{1p} + \cdots + v_{mp} \wedge w_{mp} \] So if we take the \(m\) fold wedge product \(\omega^m_p = \omega_p \wedge \cdots \wedge \omega_p\) we get \[ \omega_p = \pm m!v_{1p} \wedge w_{1p} \wedge \cdots v_{mp} \wedge w_{mp} \] where the sign is dependent on whether \(m\) is even or odd. Now \[ \omega_p(v_{1p}, w_{1p}, \dots, v_{mp}, w_{mp}) \neq 0 \] hence this is a nondegenerate form. Since this holds for all the points in the manifold we note that \(\omega^m\) is a volume form for \(M\). So we have a canonical choice of volume form called the Liouville volume form given by \(\frac{\omega^m}{m!}\). Here we have the \(m!\) for normalization reasons. This is very important because this gives us that Symplectic manifolds must be orientable. In particular, it gives a sort of nontrivial proof of the fact that for any manifold \(X\) the cotangent bundle \(T^*X\) must be orientable, since it has a canonical symplectic form defined on it.

Riemannian Manifolds

Unfortunately, unlike in the case of Lie groups and Symplectic manifolds not every Riemannian manifold is orientable. This can be seen immediately as we know that the mobius band is non orientable yet has a pullback metric on it. So let us restrict ourselves to the case of orientable manifolds. So now we may ask if there is a canonical choice of volume form? It turns out that there is. To define this, at any point \(p \in M\), choose an oriented orthonormal basis \(\{e_{1p}, \dots, e_{np}\}\) for \(T_pM\) then note that \[ \omega_p = e_{1p} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{np} \] is a volume form on \(T_pM\). Now this is well defined since if \(\{v_{1p}, \cdots, v_{np}\}\) is another oriented orthonormal basis we have a linear change of basis \(Av_{ip} = e_{ip}\) then note that \[ \begin{align*} \omega_p &= e_{1p} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{np} \\ &= (\det A)v_{1p} \wedge \cdots \wedge v_{np} \\ &= v_{1p} \wedge \cdots \wedge v_{np} \end{align*} \] hence this is independent of the choice of orthonormal basis. But how do we define this globally? Recall that an orientation is a nonvanishing section of the orienation bundle. So using the fact that the form is well defined at each point we may use this orientation section to get an orientated orthonormal basis at each point and define the volume form as such. Note that this construction does depend on the fact that \((M,g)\) is Riemannian since we are using orthonormal bases. The common notation for this volume form is \(d V_g\). Though this may not be exact the reason for this notation is that it defines a measure on compact orientable manifolds. Since we may define \[ \int_M f = \int_M f\,d V_g \] moreover we could just require that \(f\) have compact support and then the above integal is still defined. Also note that if \(M\) is compact we have the volume for \(M\) defined as \[ \int_M d V_g \] moreover this is not \(0\) since the form is nowhere vanishing. Then by Stoke's theorem the volume form is never exact on compact manifolds without boundary, since if \(d V_g\) were exact, say \(dV_g = d\eta\) then \[ \begin{align*} 0 &\neq \int_M dV_g \\ &= \int_{\partial M} \eta \\ &= 0 \end{align*} \] which is a clear contradiction. It is also important to note that this is not a construction that is unique to Riemannian manifolds, we may also do the exact same thing with volume forms defined above. It is just that it is most common to see this construction used in Riemannian geometry.

Now let us look at local expresions of this volume form. Let \(x^i\) be oriented local coordinates on \(M\). Let the cooresponding vector fields be \(\partial_i\) and the one forms be \(dx^i\). Let \(\{e_{1p}, \dots, e_{np}\}\) be an orientated orthonormal basis for \(T_pM\). Now let \(A\) be the change of basis for the coordinate frame to the oriented frame. This now means that in the coordinate frame we have \[ \omega = f\,dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^n \] and \[ \begin{align*} \omega_p &= e_{1p} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{np} \\ &= (\det A)dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^n \end{align*} \] evalutating this on \(\partial_{ip}\) we get that \(f = \det A\). Moreover note that \[ \begin{align*} g_{ij}(p) &= \langle \partial_{ip}, \partial_{jp} \rangle \\ &= \langle A_{i}^{k}e_{kp}, A_{j}^{l}e_{lp} \rangle \\ &= A_{i}^{k}A_{j}^{l}\delta_{kl} \\ &= (A^T)_{k}^{i}A_{j}^{k} \\ &= (A^TA)^{i}_{j} \end{align*} \] hence \[ \det(g_{ij}(p)) = \det(A^TA) = (\det A)^2 \] this means that \(\det A = \sqrt{\det(g_{ij}(p))}\) since \(f > 0\). This gives us that \[ \omega = \sqrt{\det(g_{ij})}\,dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^n \]

Constructions Using Volume Forms

On Riemannian manfolds this allows us to do so much now. Turns out this is the structure that is needed to be able to talk about the most beloved PDEs like the heat equation. To do this we need to define the laplacian, which if we recall needs us to define the divergence and the gradient of funcitons. So let us start, note that for the gradient, the defining property is that it is the direction of the greatest change of the function. But in the general case it is much nicer to note that the metric gives us a diffeomorphism \(TM \to T^*M\), in coordinates this cooresponds to raising the index of the components. The inverse of this map is a diffeomorphism \(T^*M \to TM\) which corresponds to lowering the indices. Now note that if \(f: M \to \R\) is smooth then \(df_p \in T_p^*M\) and hence we get a tangent vector \(\text{grad}f_p \in T_pM\). Note that this is a smooth vector field on \(M\) form the fact that the components are products and sums of smooth functions. In coordinates note that \[ df = \partial_i f\,dx^i \] and so \[ \text{grad}f = g^{ij}\partial_j f\,\partial_i \] But this does not use the volume form, so let us do that. Note that if \(X\) is a smooth vector field on a Riemannian manifold then \(d(\iota_XdV_g)\) is a top form. This means that it is a multiple of \(dV_g\) so let \(\text{div } X\) be this factor. The reason for this definition can be seen from the fact that this obeys the divergence theorem from multivariable calculus.

Divergence Theorem

Suppose \((M, g)\) is a compact orientable Riemannian manifold with boundary (though this works even when \(M\) is not orientable) then for any smooth vector field \(X \in \mathfrak{X}(M)\) and let \(N\) be the unit outward pointing normal vector field on \(\partial M\) then \[ \int_{M}(\text{div } X)\,d V_g = \int_{\partial M}g(X, N)\,i^*\iota_NdV_g \] where \(i: \partial M \to M\) is the inclusion.

Proof: This is a standard application of Stoke's Theorem, note theta \[ \begin{align*} \int_{M}(\text{div } X)\,d V_g &= \int_{M}d(\iota_XdV_g) \\ &= \int_{\partial M}i^*\iota_X dV_{g} \\ &= \int_{\partial M}g(X, N)i^*\iota_N dV_{g} \end{align*} \] We must now justify that \(i^*\iota_X dV_{g} = g(X, N)i^*\iota_N dV_{g}\). So note that tangent vectors \(Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1} \in T(\partial M)\), and an orthonormal frame \(e_1, \dots, e_{n-1}\) of \(T(\partial M)\) we have that \(N, e_1, \dots, e_n\) form an orthonromal frame of \(TM\) and \[ \begin{align*} (i^*\iota_X dV_{g})(Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}) &= dV_g(X, Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}) \\ &= dV_g\left(g(X, N)N + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}g(X, e_i)e_i, Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}\right) \\ &= g(X, N)dV_g\left(N, Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}\right)\\ &\qquad\qquad+ \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}g(X, e_i)dV_g\left(e_i, Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}\right)\\ &= g(X, N)dV_g\left(N, Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}\right) \\ &= g(X, N)(i^*\iota_NdV_g)(Y_1, \dots, Y_{n-1}) \end{align*} \] the reason the other terms vanish is because the \(Y_j\) are a linear combination of the \(e_i\).

On Riemannian manfolds, one may use the volume form to extend the definition of the Hodge star operator. More of this can be seen in the following post: Hodge star.
